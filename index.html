Research Experience
Integrating Single-Cell Morphology and Gene Expression for Cross-Modal Analysis
First author  ·  Prof. Kevin Z. Lin, University of Washington  ·  Jan. 2025 – Present
A fundamental challenge in single-cell biology is that two of the most information-rich data modalities — a cell's physical shape (morphology) and its gene expression profile — are rarely measured together on the same cells. Without paired observations, it is unclear whether morphology and transcriptomics reflect the same underlying cell states, or how to transfer information between them. To address this, we developed GeoAdvAE, a geometry-aware adversarial variational autoencoder built in PyTorch. The model jointly encodes both modalities into a shared latent space using a multi-objective loss that combines adversarial alignment with Gromov–Wasserstein optimal transport — a technique that aligns geometric structures across spaces without requiring point-to-point correspondences. On benchmark Patch-seq data, GeoAdvAE achieved roughly 20% higher cell-type alignment accuracy than 11 competing methods. We further applied it to unpaired microglia data from a mouse model of Alzheimer's disease, uncovering morphology-associated gene programs tied to disease-relevant microglial states.

STPath — Spatial Transcriptomics-Guided Deep Learning for Pathology Image Analysis
Research Assistant  ·  Prof. Wei Sun, Fred Hutchinson Cancer Center  ·  Feb. 2025 – Present
Histopathology slides contain rich spatial information about tumor microenvironments, but quantifying cell-type composition from images alone is a hard problem. Spatial transcriptomics, which measures gene expression at spatially resolved spots in a tissue section, offers a natural supervision signal. We developed an end-to-end pipeline that deconvolves cell-type proportions from spatial transcriptomics data (using a modified CARD R package with custom differentially expressed genes), then uses those proportions to supervise a downstream image model. Latent embeddings from multiple pathology vision foundation models (CONCH, ProvGigapath, Virchow, UNI2h) are extracted from the slide images, and an XGBoost model is trained to predict cell-type composition directly from image features — reaching a Pearson correlation of ~0.7 on held-out lung cancer samples.
Cohort-Level Differential Distributional Analysis of Microglia in Alzheimer's Disease
Co-author  ·  Prof. Kevin Z. Lin & UW Neuroscience  ·  Sep. 2024 – Jun. 2025
Standard differential expression (DE) analysis compares mean gene expression levels between groups — but in complex diseases like Alzheimer's, the interesting biology often lives in the shape of the expression distribution rather than its average. Microglia, the brain's immune cells, are particularly heterogeneous: the same bulk-level average can mask very different subpopulation structures across cohorts. We developed Was2CoDE, a Python framework using nonparametric Wasserstein-based metrics to detect cohort-level distributional shifts in microglial gene expression from single-nuclei RNA-seq data. We benchmarked it against classical DE methods (edgeR, DESeq2, eSVD, and an scVI-based framework) across multiple real datasets (Prater, SEA-AD, ROS-MAP), finding approximately 25% higher sensitivity for detecting subtype-specific microglial activation patterns.
Survival Modeling of Abnormal Ambulatory Blood Pressure and Kidney Function in Pediatric Patients
Research Assistant  ·  Prof. Sangeeta R. Hingorani, Seattle Children's Hospital  ·  Jul. 2024 – Sep. 2024
Pediatric patients with kidney abnormalities are followed longitudinally over years, but their clinic visits are irregular, data is often missing, and blood pressure measurements are recorded around the clock — all of which break standard longitudinal modeling assumptions. I contributed to two related nephrology studies. The first examined how nocturnal hypertension predicts future daytime hypertension in otherwise healthy children; I proposed an adaptive Cox modeling framework with time-dependent covariate updating to handle irregular follow-up and informative censoring. The second study tracked kidney function decline in children with a solitary functioning kidney, where I developed a REML-imputation hybrid mixed model for robust estimation of nonlinear eGFR trajectories. Both studies were accepted for presentation at WMRC 2026 and PAS 2026, and the work received the Best Gastroenterology, Hepatology & Nephrology Abstract Award from the Western Society for Pediatric Research.

Optimization of Healthcare Resource Allocation During the COVID-19 Pandemic
First author  ·  Prof. Jingjin Wu & Prof. Yuhui Deng  ·  Jan. 2023 – Dec. 2024
When vaccines and ventilators are both scarce and intertwined with epidemic dynamics, allocating them efficiently across heterogeneous populations is a challenging combinatorial optimization problem. We built a joint optimization framework coupling an extended SVUIR epidemiological model with a linearized mixed-integer program (MIP). Nonlinear constraints were linearized via Big-M methods to make the problem tractable at scale. Applied to real COVID-19 data across 17 age groups and 5 regions over a 400-day planning horizon, the framework achieved over 60% higher computational efficiency and up to 70% cost reduction relative to baseline heuristic strategies.
Optimization of UAV Trajectory Planning and Task Allocation in Fog Computing
Project Director  ·  Prof. Jingjin Wu & Prof. Yuhui Deng  ·  Jun. 2022 – Jan. 2024
UAVs deployed for edge/fog computing must simultaneously fly stable paths, complete assigned computational tasks, and conserve battery — objectives that conflict under real-world weather and terrain variability. We tackled this as a holistic 3D optimization problem, developing an adaptive control algorithm for flight stability and an improved ant colony optimization method for joint trajectory planning and task assignment, validated against real meteorological and terrain data. The framework achieved a ≥34% gain in energy efficiency and a 2.6× increase in task completion rate over baselines. The project was independently funded by the Provincial Government's "Climbing Plan" research grant.
